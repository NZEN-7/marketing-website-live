Thermal Storage vs Lithium Battery: Which Is Right for Your Home?

If you have solar and you've started looking at storage, you've probably been pointed straight at a lithium battery. But for most homes the more useful question is thermal storage vs lithium battery: because the two store energy for very different jobs, at very different costs. Here's the honest comparison, including where a battery genuinely wins.

Start with one fact that reframes everything: in a typical Australian home, 60–70% of the energy you actually control is thermal: heating, cooling, and hot water. Lights, the TV, the fridge, your laptop: real, but small. The big, predictable, expensive loads are about moving heat. So the real storage question isn't "battery or not", it's "what's the cheapest way to store energy for the loads that dominate my bill?"

Thermal storage vs lithium battery: where your money actually goes

A battery stores electricity. To heat your home with it, you store solar as electrons, then later turn those electrons back into heat, paying for premium battery capacity and losing a little at each conversion. Thermal storage skips the round trip: it stores your solar directly as heat (or cold), in water, ready to use.

That difference shows up starkly in the cost to store a kilowatt-hour:

The gap isn't a quirk of today's prices. Storing heat in mass is structurally cheaper than storing electrons in chemistry, and that gap is physics, it doesn't close as battery prices fall.

What batteries are genuinely good at

This isn't an anti-battery article. A battery is a fine way to run the parts of your home that need electricity after dark, lights, power points, an EV charger, keeping the fridge going in an outage. If those are your priority, a battery does real work.

What a battery is not is the cost-effective way to cover heating and hot water. Using expensive electrochemical storage to later recreate heat is overkill when the load was thermal to begin with, and heating and hot water are usually the biggest line on the bill.

The "have both" scenario

For a lot of homes the smartest answer isn't either/or. Thermal storage handles the heavy thermal lifting cheaply, and a smaller battery than you'd otherwise buy covers the residual electrical loads. You right-size each tool to the job instead of asking one expensive battery to do everything. If you're choosing one to start with, thermal storage is the one that pays back your single biggest load, your heating bill.

Why no degradation matters more than it sounds

A lithium battery loses capacity every cycle; in about a decade you're planning a replacement. A water-based thermal store has no charge-cycle limit and no capacity fade, the same tank doing the job today will be doing it in twenty years. No fire risk, no chemistry to manage, no replacement timer. For an asset you're buying to cut bills for decades, that longevity is part of the economics, not a footnote.

The bottom line

Most homes don't need a battery so much as they need somewhere to put their solar before it leaves the building. For the loads that dominate your bill, thermal storage stores energy far more cheaply, lasts far longer, and carries none of the fire or degradation risk. Batteries still earn their place for electrical loads, and some homes will sensibly run both. The point isn't to be against batteries. It's to match the cheapest storage to your biggest load.
